The Internet — The Invisible Engine of Modern Operations

Long before instant messages, online shopping, and digital collaboration, the world moved at a very different pace. Before the Internet reshaped modern life, organizations worked in a world defined by distance and delay. Factories still turned raw materials into finished goods, and businesses still served customers, but the way everything connected was slow and fragmented. Information moved through paper documents, phone calls, and physical approvals. When something went wrong in production or supply, managers often found out too late to respond quickly. The system worked, but it was rigid, delayed, and heavily dependent on manual coordination. It was a world waiting for something invisible to change everything. Then, without most people fully realizing it at first, a new kind of infrastructure began to emerge.

The Birth of the Invisible Network

Our tale truly begins in 1969 in the United States. In the late 20th century, a growing network of connected computers started linking institutions and researchers. What seemed like a simple communication tool quickly evolved into something far more powerful: the Internet. At first, it was used for exchanging messages and sharing data. But beneath this surface-level function, it was quietly changing something much deeper: the way organizations were structured and operated. This is where a major shift in operations management began: Business Process Re-engineering (BPR). Instead of simply improving existing processes, BPR focuses on completely redesigning how work is done in order to achieve dramatic improvements in speed, efficiency, and performance. The Internet became the catalyst for this transformation. Gradually, organizations began to rethink their entire workflows. Processes that once relied on paper forms were replaced with digital systems. Approval chains that used to take days were compressed into instant online decisions. Departments that once worked in isolation were now connected in real time. The Internet did not just make communication faster; it allowed businesses to completely redesign how work moved through the organization.

When the Network Grew

As this invisible re-engineering continued, supply chains also began to transform. Suppliers, manufacturers, and retailers could now share information instantly, allowing production and distribution systems to operate in sync. Instead of reacting slowly to delays or shortages, companies could adjust operations in real time. What used to be a linear, delayed system became a coordinated, responsive network. At the same time, quality and performance management improved because processes were now visible and measurable. Managers could track operations as they happened, identify inefficiencies immediately, and redesign workflows continuously. This shift reflected the core idea of BPR: not improving broken processes, but rebuilding them entirely for better outcomes. Even areas like product development changed. Teams no longer worked in isolated stages; instead, designers, engineers, and managers could collaborate simultaneously through digital platforms. This reduced development time and eliminated unnecessary steps in traditional workflows, another clear outcome of process re-engineering.

The Quiet Transformation of Work

Over time, what began as a communication tool became something much more powerful: an invisible system that reshaped how organizations function. The Internet did not simply support operations; it forced them to be redesigned from the ground up. Today, most organizations no longer notice this transformation because it has become normal. Yet the reality is that modern operations are built on layers of re-engineered processes; faster, more connected, and more efficient than anything that existed before. In this sense, the Internet is not just a technological innovation. It is one of the greatest examples of Business Process Re-engineering in history, quietly reshaping how the world works by redesigning the very processes that keep it running.
