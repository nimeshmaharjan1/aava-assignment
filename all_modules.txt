<div class="headline"> The Tale of the Guiding Stone
</div>

<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">L</span>ong ago, in ancient China, sometime between the 2nd century BC and the 1st century AD, curious scholars discovered something unusual. They found a strange black stone called lodestone. Whenever they placed it carefully on a surface where it could move freely, it would slowly turn… always pointing in the same direction: north and south.
</p>
  
  <p>At first, people did not use this mysterious stone to travel. Instead, it was used for geomancy, a form of divination. Over time, however, people began experimenting with the stone and discovered that it could consistently point in one direction. This simple observation eventually led to the creation of the magnetic compass, a tool that would transform navigation and global exploration.

</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">From Ancient Curiosity to Navigational Innovation</div>
  <p>The story of the compass is not simply the story of an invention, but also a story of innovation. Rather than appearing suddenly as a finished product, the compass developed gradually through continuous improvements over many centuries. Early versions used floating magnetic needles in water, while later designs introduced dry compasses that were more practical for long sea voyages. This process reflects incremental innovation, where existing technology is continuously refined and improved over time.
The compass also demonstrates architectural innovation. Although the magnetic principle behind the lodestone remained the same, its purpose changed dramatically. What began as a tool connected to spiritual beliefs and geomancy was later reconfigured into a navigation instrument that sailors could use to cross oceans. By applying existing knowledge in a new way, societies transformed the compass into a technology that reshaped travel, trade, and exploration.

</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">An Idea That Crossed Oceans and Changed the World </div>
  <p>As the compass became more useful, knowledge about it spread beyond China through merchants, travelers, and sailors. Different cultures adopted the technology, improved its design, and adapted it to their own needs. This reflects the concept of open innovation, where ideas and knowledge are shared across societies rather than remaining within one isolated group. The collaboration and exchange of knowledge across regions accelerated the development and global impact of the compass.
With the help of the compass, sailors no longer relied only on the stars or visible coastlines to navigate. Long-distance sea travel became safer and more reliable, allowing trade routes to expand and connecting different parts of the world more closely than ever before. Explorers used navigation tools like the compass to travel across unknown oceans, creating new economic opportunities and cultural exchanges. However, these advancements also contributed to imperial expansion and colonization, as powerful nations used improved navigation technologies to reach and control distant territories.
From a simple magnetic stone to a revolutionary navigation tool, the compass illustrates how innovation evolves through experimentation, adaptation, and the sharing of knowledge. Its story demonstrates how incremental improvements, new applications of existing technologies, and open innovation can shape human history and transform the world.

</p>

<div class="headline">THE TALE OF BLUETOOTH: A SWEDISH KING AND A WIRELESS REVOLUTION</div>

<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">I</span>magine it's the mid-1990s. In 1994, engineers Jaap Haartsen and Sven Mattisson at Sweden's Ericsson tackled a persistent challenge: eliminating wired connections and cables between mobile devices. The goal was to strategically develop this innovation and standardize it across industries to turn it into a global connectivity standard.</p>
  
  <p>The name "Bluetooth" was proposed in 1997, inspired by the 10th-century Scandinavian king, Harald "Bluetooth" Gormsson, who successfully united Denmark and Norway. The creators chose this name to symbolize the technology's ability to unite the fragmented worlds of PC and cellular industries! A fascinating part of this history remains in every smartphone today: the iconic Bluetooth logo is actually a Viking bindrune, merging the runes for Harald's initials: Hagall and Bjarkan.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Navigating Pearson's Map</div>
  <p>Managing the innovation process fundamentally requires managing uncertainty. According to <b>Pearson's Uncertainty Map</b>, projects are evaluated based on their uncertainty regarding the process and the output. Bluetooth is a textbook example of Quadrant 3: <b>Applications Engineering</b>. While they were trying to solve a market problem, the underlying dynamic is about technical versus market uncertainty. Ericsson already possessed deep engineering capabilities in short-range radio frequencies from their mobile phone development (meaning low uncertainty about the process). However, creating a "Personal Area Network" to replace cables was an entirely new concept. There was high uncertainty about the output because the commercial market for such connectivity did not yet exist. The firm's challenge was taking a known technology and finding a viable commercial application.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The Ambidexterity Dilemma</div>
  <p>Developing Bluetooth required Ericsson to navigate a core management dilemma: balancing the need for efficiency with the need for creativity. To achieve this, the firm had to practice organizational <b>Ambidexterity</b>&mdash;the ability to integrate both incremental and radical approaches within the same organization. On one hand, Ericsson relied on <b>Exploitation</b> for its primary mobile phone manufacturing business, which requires stable routines and controlled environments to maximize efficiency. On the other hand, bringing the Bluetooth project to life required <b>Exploration</b>. This demanded a more flexible environment with enough organizational "slack" to allow engineers the creative freedom to test new ideas and standards.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Avoiding the Myopic Trap</div>
  <p>A major risk in managing innovation is developing a <b>Myopic View</b>&mdash;becoming too heavily focused on the technology itself while ignoring customer needs and broader market dynamics. If Ericsson had kept Bluetooth as a closed, proprietary system, it likely would have failed, because communication networks rely entirely on widespread compatibility. To avoid this trap, Ericsson prioritized external links and receptivity. In 1998, they formed the Bluetooth Special Interest Group (SIG) with partners like IBM, Intel, Nokia, and Toshiba. By relinquishing exclusive control and opting for an open global standard, they shifted their focus from protecting a proprietary technology to driving mass market adoption, ensuring Bluetooth became a foundational, everyday standard.</p>
</div>

<div class="headline"> The Internet — The Invisible Engine of Modern Operations

</div>


<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">L</span>ong before instant messages, online shopping, and digital collaboration, the world moved at a very different pace.
Before the Internet reshaped modern life, organizations worked in a world defined by distance and delay. Factories still turned raw materials into finished goods, and businesses still served customers, but the way everything connected was slow and fragmented. Information moved through paper documents, phone calls, and physical approvals. When something went wrong in production or supply, managers often found out too late to respond quickly. The system worked, but it was rigid, delayed, and heavily dependent on manual coordination. It was a world waiting for something invisible to change everything. Then, without most people fully realizing it at first, a new kind of infrastructure began to emerge.


</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The Birth of the Invisible Network</div>
  
  <p>Our tale truly begins in 1969 in the United States.
In the late 20th century, a growing network of connected computers started linking institutions and researchers. What seemed like a simple communication tool quickly evolved into something far more powerful: the Internet. At first, it was used for exchanging messages and sharing data. But beneath this surface-level function, it was quietly changing something much deeper: the way organizations were structured and operated. This is where a major shift in operations management began: Business Process Re-engineering (BPR). Instead of simply improving existing processes, BPR focuses on completely redesigning how work is done in order to achieve dramatic improvements in speed, efficiency, and performance. The Internet became the catalyst for this transformation. Gradually, organizations began to rethink their entire workflows. Processes that once relied on paper forms were replaced with digital systems. Approval chains that used to take days were compressed into instant online decisions. Departments that once worked in isolation were now connected in real time. The Internet did not just make communication faster; it allowed businesses to completely redesign how work moved through the organization.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading"> When the Network Grew </div>
  <p>As this invisible re-engineering continued, supply chains also began to transform. Suppliers, manufacturers, and retailers could now share information instantly, allowing production and distribution systems to operate in sync. Instead of reacting slowly to delays or shortages, companies could adjust operations in real time. What used to be a linear, delayed system became a coordinated, responsive network. At the same time, quality and performance management improved because processes were now visible and measurable. Managers could track operations as they happened, identify inefficiencies immediately, and redesign workflows continuously. This shift reflected the core idea of BPR: not improving broken processes, but rebuilding them entirely for better outcomes. Even areas like product development changed. Teams no longer worked in isolated stages; instead, designers, engineers, and managers could collaborate simultaneously through digital platforms. This reduced development time and eliminated unnecessary steps in traditional workflows, another clear outcome of process re-engineering. 
</p>
 <div class="sub-heading"> The Quiet Transformation of Work </div>
  <p>Over time, what began as a communication tool became something much more powerful: an invisible system that reshaped how organizations function. The Internet did not simply support operations; it forced them to be redesigned from the ground up. Today, most organizations no longer notice this transformation because it has become normal. Yet the reality is that modern operations are built on layers of re-engineered processes; faster, more connected, and more efficient than anything that existed before. In this sense, the Internet is not just a technological innovation. It is one of the greatest examples of Business Process Re-engineering in history, quietly reshaping how the world works by redesigning the very processes that keep it running.
</p>

<div class="headline">FROM NOTHING, EVERYTHING: HOW ZERO CHANGED THE WORLD</div>


<div class="content-columns">

  <div class="sub-heading">The Number That Shouldn't Exist — And Yet Built Everything</div>
  <p><span class="drop-cap">B</span>efore zero, humanity only counted what it could see. When it finally arrived from the Indian subcontinent, it changed how humanity organises, transfers, and builds on knowledge. Examined through the lens of Knowledge and Innovation Management, the story of zero is not just mathematical history. It is a masterclass in technology trajectories, absorptive capacity, knowledge transfer, and the cyclic nature of innovation.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">A Dominant Design Emerging from a Fluid Phase</div>
  <p>In 628 AD, the Indian mathematician Brahmagupta formally defined zero as a number with arithmetic rules in his treatise Brahmasphutasiddhanta. Earlier cultures had approached the concept: Babylonians used a placeholder around 300 BC, and the Maya developed their own zero independently around 350 AD. Yet none established a dominant design.</p>
  <p>This mirrors precisely what the Abernathy-Utterback model describes as the fluid phase of innovation — a period characterised by an explosion of competing designs and radical product innovation, before any single solution emerges as the standard. Multiple civilisations were experimenting with representations of nothingness, but the field remained unsettled. Brahmagupta's formalisation and the earliest physical inscription at the Chaturbhuj Temple, Gwalior (876 AD) marked the transition toward a dominant design: the Hindu-Arabic numeral system that would eventually become universal.</p>
  <p>Once adopted, innovation shifted from the product itself toward process: how to compute with it, how to teach it, how to embed it in commerce and science. This is the Abernathy-Utterback transitional phase, where process innovation overtakes product experimentation.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Technology Trajectories and the Limits of the Possible</div>
  <p>Zero did not spring from one mind in isolation. Indian astronomy demanded precision in tracking planetary positions, exposing systematic gaps that existing number systems could not handle. The technology trajectory of Indian mathematics — its accumulated capability in positional notation, astronomical calculation, and philosophical abstraction — made zero achievable within that context.</p>
  <p>As the course material defines it: "The technology capability of the firm frequently dictates what is possible and which innovations can or cannot be achieved in a given time frame." Hindu and Buddhist philosophy, which embraced shunya (Sanskrit for void) as a metaphysical reality, was not merely cultural background — it was part of the knowledge base that made the innovation possible. Individual assets, philosophical traditions, and accumulated scientific practice combined to form an organisational knowledge base that rivals simply did not possess.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Absorptive Capacity: Why Some Civilisations Could Adopt Zero and Others Could Not</div>
  <p>The transmission of zero from India to the wider world was not automatic. It required absorptive capacity — the ability to access external knowledge and subsequently disseminate it effectively within a receiving system.</p>
  <p>In 9th-century Baghdad, the Persian scholar Al-Khwarizmi synthesised Indian numerical ideas into new frameworks, laying the foundations of algebra and the concept of algorithms. His success was no accident: the House of Wisdom maintained a deep existing knowledge base in mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy. It possessed the prior knowledge required to recognise, internalise, and extend what it encountered — the hallmark of high absorptive capacity.</p>
  <p>Using the 4A framework of technology transfer, the process unfolds clearly: Awareness — Islamic scholars encountered Indian mathematical texts through trade and diplomatic contact. Association — Al-Khwarizmi and his contemporaries recognised the value of positional notation for their existing scientific and administrative needs. Assimilation — Resources were committed, including translation programmes and institutional support at the House of Wisdom, creating genuine organisational commitment to the new system. Application — The knowledge was operationalised in algebra, astronomy, and eventually in commercial calculation across the Islamic world.</p>
  <p>When Fibonacci introduced Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe in his Liber Abaci (1202), he was performing the same transfer into a new organisational context.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The NIH Syndrome and the Cost of Resistance</div>
  <p>European resistance to Arabic numerals over several centuries is a textbook case of the Not-Invented-Here (NIH) syndrome — the tendency to reject external innovations in favour of in-house solutions, often rooted in distrust of external sources or institutional inertia. Roman numerals were familiar, embedded in administrative systems, and carried the authority of tradition. Adopting a system originating outside European scholarly tradition required overcoming exactly the kind of cultural and institutional resistance that NIH describes.</p>
  <p>The cost was measurable: delayed advancement in trade calculation, scientific notation, and engineering. A dominant incumbent dismissed an external innovation as unnecessary, only to face accelerating competitive pressure once adoption tipped.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Dynamic Capabilities and Knowledge That Can Crash a System</div>
  <p>Zero's dark technical property is that division by zero yields an undefined, indeterminate result. Even Brahmagupta initially got this wrong. In computing, an unhandled division-by-zero crashes the program. The number representing nothing, mismanaged, can bring down everything.</p>
  <p>The concept maps precisely onto dynamic capabilities: the ability to reconfigure a firm's resources and routines in response to changing conditions. Organisations that lack the dynamic capability to manage their own knowledge gaps — to recognise what they do not know, and to act before that gap becomes a structural failure — replicate exactly this problem.</p>

</div>

<div class="headline">STRATEGIC R&D MANAGEMENT: BEYOND THE LUCK OF THE MIRACLE MOLD</div>

<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">W</span>hile historical narratives often attribute the discovery of Penicillin in 1928 solely to luck, a critical R&D management perspective reveals a far more complex reality of strategic decision making under uncertainty. Alexander Fleming's initial observation was a classic example of basic research, occupying the earliest stage of the R&D continuum where the focus is strictly on knowledge and concepts. At this phase, product tangibility was at its lowest, and the discovery was merely a scientific idea without a clear path to becoming a physical product.</p>
  
  <p>The transition from a laboratory anomaly to a medical standard was hindered by the severe lack of available information regarding its potential, a common obstacle that makes project evaluation difficult even for well resourced organizations. Consequently, Penicillin faced the high drop out rates typical of early R&D project ideas, where many promising concepts fail to pass through the initial gates of technical and financial feasibility.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Moving Down the R&D Continuum</div>
  <p>The true innovation occurred when the project shifted from basic research into applied research and development. In the late 1930s, the team led by Howard Florey and Ernst Chain took on the role of product champions, fighting for the investment and resources required to develop the compound into a usable medicine. Their efforts focused on overcoming the innovation dilemma: the tension between exploring new scientific frontiers and the need for successful commercialization.</p>

  <p>This phase required rigorous project evaluation using specific criteria such as technical success, manufacturing stability, and strategic fit. By transforming the mold from a knowledge concept into a tangible solution, they navigated the R&D continuum toward high product tangibility, proving that mass production was technically viable.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">A Breakthrough Project Success</div>
  <p>According to R&D project mapping, the industrialization of Penicillin represents a breakthrough project, involving the creation of an entirely new core product and the development of radical new core processes. This success was not a result of serendipity alone but of a structured R&D strategic decision making process. It demonstrates that while basic research provides the foundation, only the systematic management of the R&D continuum and the support of dedicated product champions can deliver a successful product to the global market.</p>

</div>

<div class="headline">Radio Waves to Mass Market: An Italian Visionary's Triumph</div>

<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">I</span>n 1894, a 20-year-old Italian named Guglielmo Marconi began experimenting with electromagnetic waves in the attic of his family's country estate in Bologna. Despite having no formal education in engineering, he successfully transmitted a wireless signal over a mile and a half, bypassing the physical barrier of a hill. Unable to find funding in Italy, he traveled to London and filed the world's first patent for a wireless telegraphy system in 1896. A fascinating historical fact: Marconi's wireless apparatus is widely credited with saving over 700 lives during the sinking of the <i>Titanic</i> in 1912, as the technology allowed the ship to send distress calls over the ocean rather than relying on carrier pigeons.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Riding the S-Curve of Diffusion</div>
  <p>The journey of the radio is a perfect historical illustration of Everett Rogers' <b>Innovation Diffusion Theory</b>. Technology diffusion concerns how an innovation is communicated over time within a wider social system. The adoption of the radio followed a classic <b>S-Curve</b>. The initial <b>Innovators</b> were not everyday consumers, but rather maritime shipping companies and military organizations that recognized the extreme advantage of ship-to-shore communication. Next came the <b>Early Adopters</b>&mdash;amateur radio enthusiasts and engineers who built their own crystal sets in their garages. The explosive leap into the steep part of the <b>S-Curve</b> (the <b>Early Majority</b>) occurred in 1920, catalyzed by Frank Conrad, an engineer at Westinghouse. Conrad had been independently broadcasting popular air concerts from his home garage, prompting a local department store to start selling $10 at home radio receivers so people could listen to him. Capitalizing on this consumer interest, Westinghouse established station KDKA in Pittsburgh and made the first scheduled commercial broadcast on November 2, 1920, airing live presidential election returns. Once networks began providing scheduled programming&mdash;like live sports, music, and news&mdash;the radio rapidly penetrated the majority of households, transforming from a niche scientific tool into a cultural necessity.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Crossing the Chasm</div>
  <p>To achieve this widespread diffusion, the radio had to successfully navigate Geoffrey Moore's concept of <b>Crossing the Chasm</b>. There is a significant gap between <b>Early Adopters</b> (<b>Visionaries</b> and technology enthusiasts) and the <b>Early Majority</b> (<b>Pragmatists</b>). The <b>Visionaries</b> were fascinated by the raw technology of wireless transmission itself. However, the <b>Pragmatists</b> did not care about the underlying physics or tinkering with transmitters; they required a complete, user-friendly solution that offered clear value. The radio crossed this chasm by shifting its fundamental business model. It moved from being a complex, two-way, point-to-point communication device to a simple, one-way broadcast medium that provided free entertainment directly to the consumer.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Characteristics Driving Adoption</div>
  <p>The rapid diffusion of the radio was driven by specific characteristics that facilitate market adoption. It offered a massive <b>Relative Advantage</b> over newspapers by providing instantaneous, live information. Furthermore, its high <b>Observability</b> acted as a catalyst for social contagion. Because a radio could be heard playing in public spaces, shop windows, or a neighbor's home, the innovation was highly visible (and audible) to non-adopters. This natural observability broke down behavioral barriers to diffusion, fueling exponential sales and accelerating its spread across the globe.</p>

<div class="headline">WINGS OF STRATEGY: THE INTEGRATED R&D JOURNEY OF THE AIRPLANE</div>

<div class="content-columns">
  <p><span class="drop-cap">T</span>he successful flight of the Wright brothers in 1903 represents the ultimate subset of innovation management: the transformation of a business opportunity into a tangible physical product. While often viewed as a singular moment of luck, a professional analysis reveals a sophisticated New Product Development process that navigates the core tensions of R&D management.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Strategic Positioning and the Ansoff Matrix</div>
  <p>From a strategic perspective, the development of the airplane was a high stakes diversification strategy. According to the Ansoff matrix, the brothers were not merely improving an existing product but creating a new to the world innovation for a market that did not yet exist. This required a profound understanding of NPD strategy, focusing on long term growth and renewal rather than short term efficiency gains.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Managing the Dilemma and Uncertainty</div>
  <p>The brothers faced the fundamental dilemma of innovation management: the greater the effort to innovate, the greater the likelihood of failure, yet the failure to innovate would have meant missing the boat entirely. On Pearson's uncertainty map, their early work resided in the exploratory research quadrant, where uncertainty about both the technical process and the final output was at its peak. To manage this, they utilized dynamic capabilities, reconfiguring their bicycle shop routines into a specialized R&D unit.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The R&D Continuum and Product Tangibility</div>
  <p>Their journey followed the R&D continuum precisely. It began with basic research into the knowledge and concepts of lift and drag. As they built gliders, they moved into applied research, where technical service and engineering knowledge were applied to solve specific control problems. Finally, through iterative development, they reached high product tangibility with the 1903 Flyer.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Organizational Enablers and Key Individuals</div>
  <p>The Wright brothers operated within an organic organizational structure, characterized by open channels of communication and authority based on expertise rather than formal positions. In this team, they fulfilled multiple key individual roles: they were the technical innovators generating new ideas, the product champions selling their vision to skeptical military gatekeepers, and the project leaders balancing technical goals with organizational needs.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Models of NPD and Decision Gates</div>
  <p>Their methodology mirrored the linear NPD model, starting with rigorous idea generation and screening. Their approach also resembled a network model, accumulating knowledge from external scientific inputs and competitor failures. Each testing season acted as a decision stage or gate, where they had to evaluate technical feasibility and financial suitability before proceeding.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Conclusion and Technical Viability</div>
  <p>The airplane was a breakthrough project that required a total reconfiguration of existing technology. By ensuring technological viability through wind tunnel testing and commercial viability through government contracts, they navigated the entire R&D strategic decision making process. Their success proves that breakthrough innovation is a manageable strategic process rather than a singular event of chance.</p>

<div class="headline">THE BEEP THAT SHOOK THE WORLD</div>

<div class="content-columns">

  <p><span class="drop-cap">S</span>putnik 1 was, in product terms, simple: a metal sphere, four antennae, a radio pulse. What made it remarkable was not the object but the development system behind it — and the system it lacked. Examined through the NPD process, Sputnik is a case study in what a closed knowledge network can achieve once, and why it cannot sustain that achievement.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The Network Model: Strong Inside, Blind Outside</div>
  <p>The network model of NPD places product development at the intersection of internal functions — R&D, engineering, manufacturing, marketing, and finance — fed continuously by external inputs: competitors, suppliers, universities, and customers. Knowledge accumulates at the centre. The richer the external connections, the more resilient the innovation.</p>

  <p>Sputnik's development network was internally formidable but externally hollow. R&D and engineering were tightly integrated and world-class. But there were no customer feedback channels, no open university exchange, no supplier partnerships in any meaningful sense. The network produced a first-generation product with precision. It had no mechanism to grow outward as product complexity increased. Each subsequent mission placed greater strain on a system that was not designed to learn from outside itself.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Linked Activities: Where the Feedback Loop Broke</div>
  <p>The NPD process is a series of linked, iterative activities: assembling knowledge, generating opportunities, developing concepts, building prototypes, testing, and introducing to market. Screening and evaluation are not a one-off gate — they operate continuously at every stage, feeding information back into the cycle.</p>

  <p>Sputnik's early development followed this model well. Knowledge was accumulated over years. The product concept was precise. Prototyping and testing were rigorous. But the feedback loop broke at the evaluation stage. Failures were classified rather than incorporated. Testing data that contradicted programme ambitions was suppressed rather than used to reshape the concept. The iterative process became linear by political decree. Without continuous evaluation feeding back into development, the NPD cycle stalled — silently, and at great cost.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">Product Failure: Three Reasons the Programme Could Not Sustain Itself</div>
  <p>The NPD literature identifies thirteen reasons new products fail. Three apply directly here. First, poor match with organisational capabilities at scale. Sputnik was achievable within a single, centralised bureau. Later missions required coordination across competing institutions that had never been designed to collaborate. Internal organisational problems — poor cross-functional communication — were not incidental. They were structural, built into the very secrecy that had enabled the first success.</p>

  <p>Second, loss of top management support. The programme's chief designer held the entire knowledge network together through personal authority. When he died, no succession existed. The product champion — the figure the NPD process depends on to maintain direction through the development cycle — was gone, and so was the coherence he provided.</p>

  <p>Third, and most fundamentally, the absence of societal and market feedback as a driver of development. The NPD model places societal needs at the outer ring of the cycle, feeding continuously inward. Sputnik's programme replaced that input entirely with political prestige. When the prestige objective shifted, the development system had nothing else to draw upon. A process that had never listened to the outside world could not adapt when the world moved on.</p>

  <div class="sub-heading">The Lesson in the Silence</div>
  <p>Sputnik succeeded because it was simple enough for a closed system to deliver. The NPD network model, the iterative feedback loop, and the product failure taxonomy together explain why that success could not be repeated at greater complexity. The beep was the sound of a product working. The silence that followed was the sound of a process that had never been built to last.</p>
