THE TALE OF BLUETOOTH: A SWEDISH KING AND A WIRELESS REVOLUTION

Imagine it's the mid-1990s. In 1994, engineers Jaap Haartsen and Sven Mattisson at Sweden's Ericsson tackled a persistent challenge: eliminating wired connections and cables between mobile devices. The goal was to strategically develop this innovation and standardize it across industries to turn it into a global connectivity standard.

The name "Bluetooth" was proposed in 1997, inspired by the 10th-century Scandinavian king, Harald "Bluetooth" Gormsson, who successfully united Denmark and Norway. The creators chose this name to symbolize the technology's ability to unite the fragmented worlds of PC and cellular industries! A fascinating part of this history remains in every smartphone today: the iconic Bluetooth logo is actually a Viking bindrune, merging the runes for Harald's initials: Hagall and Bjarkan.

Navigating Pearson's Map

Managing the innovation process fundamentally requires managing uncertainty. According to Pearson's Uncertainty Map, projects are evaluated based on their uncertainty regarding the process and the output. Bluetooth is a textbook example of Quadrant 3: Applications Engineering. While they were trying to solve a market problem, the underlying dynamic is about technical versus market uncertainty. Ericsson already possessed deep engineering capabilities in short-range radio frequencies from their mobile phone development (meaning low uncertainty about the process). However, creating a "Personal Area Network" to replace cables was an entirely new concept. There was high uncertainty about the output because the commercial market for such connectivity did not yet exist. The firm's challenge was taking a known technology and finding a viable commercial application.

The Ambidexterity Dilemma

Developing Bluetooth required Ericsson to navigate a core management dilemma: balancing the need for efficiency with the need for creativity. To achieve this, the firm had to practice organizational Ambidexterity—the ability to integrate both incremental and radical approaches within the same organization. On one hand, Ericsson relied on Exploitation for its primary mobile phone manufacturing business, which requires stable routines and controlled environments to maximize efficiency. On the other hand, bringing the Bluetooth project to life required Exploration. This demanded a more flexible environment with enough organizational "slack" to allow engineers the creative freedom to test new ideas and standards.

Avoiding the Myopic Trap

A major risk in managing innovation is developing a Myopic View—becoming too heavily focused on the technology itself while ignoring customer needs and broader market dynamics. If Ericsson had kept Bluetooth as a closed, proprietary system, it likely would have failed, because communication networks rely entirely on widespread compatibility. To avoid this trap, Ericsson prioritized external links and receptivity. In 1998, they formed the Bluetooth Special Interest Group (SIG) with partners like IBM, Intel, Nokia, and Toshiba. By relinquishing exclusive control and opting for an open global standard, they shifted their focus from protecting a proprietary technology to driving mass market adoption, ensuring Bluetooth became a foundational, everyday standard.
